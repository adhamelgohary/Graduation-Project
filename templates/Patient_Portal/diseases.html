Heart failure

What is Heart failure?

Over View

Heart failure occurs when the heart cannot pump blood effectively, leading to fluid buildup in the lungs and shortness of breath. Conditions like narrowed arteries and high blood pressure can weaken or stiffen the heart over time. While treatment and lifestyle changes, such as weight loss, exercise, and stress management, can improve symptoms and longevity, heart failure can still be life-threatening. In severe cases, a heart transplant or a mechanical device may be needed. It is also known as congestive heart failure.

Symptoms

Symptoms may develop slowly. Sometimes, heart failure symptoms start suddenly. Heart failure symptoms may include:

  • Shortness of breath with activity or when lying down.
  • Fatigue and weakness.
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles and feet.
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat.
  • Reduced ability to exercise.
  • Wheezing.
  • A cough that doesn't go away or a cough that brings up white or pink mucus with spots of blood.
  • Swelling of the belly area.
  • Very rapid weight gain from fluid buildup.
  • Nausea and lack of appetite.
  • Difficulty concentrating or decreased alertness.
  • Chest pain if heart failure is caused by a heart attack.

Heart failure affects approximately 6.2 million adults in the United States

What are the complications that will appear due to Heart failure?

  • Liver damage. Heart failure can cause fluid buildup that puts too much pressure on the liver. This fluid backup can lead to scarring, which makes it more difficult for the liver to work properly.
  • Kidney damage or failure. Heart failure can reduce the blood flow to the kidneys. Untreated, this can cause kidney failure. Kidney damage from heart failure can require dialysis for treatment.
  • Sudden cardiac death. If the heart is weak, there is a risk of dying suddenly due to a dangerous irregular heart rhythm.
  • Other heart changes. Heart failure can cause changes in the heart's size and function. These changes may damage heart valves and cause irregular heartbeats.

Treatment plan

Full treatment plan

LAB Testing

Blood Tests

  • B-type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) or NT-proBNP: Measures heart stress and fluid overload
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC): Checks for anemia or infection.
  • Electrolytes & Kidney Function (Creatinine, BUN, Sodium, Potassium): Evaluates kidney function and electrolyte balance.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Checks for liver congestion due to heart failure.
  • Thyroid Function Tests (T3, T4, TSH): Detects thyroid-related heart problems
  • Lipid Profile: Assesses cholesterol levels to evaluate heart disease risk
  • Blood Sugar (HbA1c, Fasting Glucose): Checks for diabetes, a common risk factor.
  • Troponin Test: Detects heart muscle damage, often after a heart attack.

Imaging & Scans

  • Chest X-ray: Identifies heart enlargement and fluid buildup in the lungs
  • Echocardiogram (Echo): Uses ultrasound to assess heart size, pumping ability, and valve function.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG): Records heart rhythms and detects abnormalities
  • Cardiac MRI: Provides a detailed image of heart structure and function.
  • CT Scan: Detects coronary artery disease or blockages
  • Coronary Angiography: Uses contrast dye and X-ray to visualize blood flow in coronary arteries.
  • Stress Test (Treadmill or Nuclear Scan): Evaluates heart performance under exertion.
